# Why Exactly Four: Theoretical Rationale for the Four-Factor Structure

## Purpose

This note explains how the instrument's four rating dimensions — Sentience, Agency, Empathy, and Protectiveness — were derived. The four factors are not taken ready-made from any single source. Rather, they are a synthesis of two established frameworks in social psychology: the two-dimensional model of mind perception (Gray, Gray, & Wegner, 2007) and the tripartite model of attitude structure (Eagly & Chaiken, 1993), joined by the theoretical bridge between perceived experience and moral concern (Gray, Young, & Waytz, 2012).

## Background

**Mind perception has two dimensions.** Gray, Gray, and Wegner (2007) asked participants to compare the mental capacities of a wide range of characters — adults, infants, animals, robots, God — and factor-analysed the ratings. Two dimensions emerged. *Experience* is the perceived capacity to feel: pain, fear, hunger, pleasure, and similar states. *Agency* is the perceived capacity to do: to plan, exercise self-control, and be held responsible. In plain terms, when people judge whether something "has a mind," they are really answering two separate questions: *can it feel?* and *can it think?*

**Attitudes have three components.** Eagly and Chaiken (1993) synthesised the classical view that an attitude toward an object expresses itself through three channels: *cognition* (what I believe about it), *affect* (what I feel toward it), and *behaviour* (what I am inclined to do about it). A complete measure of a person's attitude should therefore sample all three, because the components can diverge — one may believe an entity is sophisticated yet feel nothing for it, or feel much and still do nothing.

**Perceiving experience drives moral concern.** The bridge between the two frameworks comes from later work on mind perception and morality. Gray, Young, and Waytz (2012) argue that attributing *experience* to an entity is what makes it a moral patient — something that can be wronged and therefore merits protection. This gives a principled reason to expect that judgments about an entity's mind (the first framework) should feed into feelings and actions toward it (the second), rather than the two being unrelated lists of questions.

## The synthesis

The four factors are produced by crossing these frameworks so that each component of attitude is represented, and the cognitive component is unpacked along the two known dimensions of mind perception:

| Factor | Question | Framework source | Attitude component |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sentience | Can it feel? | Experience dimension (Gray et al., 2007) | Cognition (belief about the object) |
| Agency | Can it think? | Agency dimension (Gray et al., 2007) | Cognition (belief about the object) |
| Empathy | Do I care? | Affective component (Eagly & Chaiken, 1993) | Affect (rater's feeling) |
| Protectiveness | Will I act? | Behavioural component (Eagly & Chaiken, 1993) | Behavioural intention (rater's disposition) |

Two design choices deserve explicit statement. First, the four factors deliberately mix two *kinds* of judgment: Sentience and Agency describe perceived properties of the rated entity, whereas Empathy and Protectiveness describe the rater's own stance toward it. This asymmetry is intentional — it follows the theoretical claim that perception (of experience especially) is upstream of concern and action (Gray et al., 2012) — but the two halves should not be treated as interchangeable measures of the same construct.

Second, Protectiveness measures behavioural *intention* (willingness to act), not behaviour itself. The distinction matters: the attitude literature documents a persistent gap between what people say they would do and what they do (Eagly & Chaiken, 1993). Claims based on this factor should be phrased accordingly.

## What the sources do and do not license

The cited works validate the individual ingredients: the Experience/Agency split is a replicated empirical finding, and the cognition/affect/behaviour structure is a standard account of attitudes. Neither source, however, proposes or validates this specific four-factor instrument. The combination is original to this project, so its psychometric properties (factor separation, reliability, convergent validity) are an empirical question for the collected data, not something the citations settle in advance.
